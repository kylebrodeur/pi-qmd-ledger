### 2. Orchestration & Architecture Patterns

Modern orchestration has moved toward five distinct architectural patterns, often combined into hybrid systems:

*   **Hierarchical (Tree):** A "Root" agent decomposes goals into sub-tasks for "Managers," who delegate to "Workers." *Best for: Context window management in massive enterprise systems.*
*   **Supervisor (Hub-and-Spoke):** A central supervisor maintains state, selects workers, and validates output. Frequently uses **SLM/LLM hybrids** (e.g., a fast Phi-4 model routing to a heavy GPT-4o).
*   **Blackboard (Shared State):** Agents monitor a central data repository (often a vector DB or Redis) and contribute asynchronously. *Best for: Non-linear collaboration like multi-step manufacturing design.*
*   **Peer-to-Peer (MCP-Enabled):** Agents discover and negotiate with each other directly using the **Model Context Protocol (MCP)**, treating other agents as "tools."
*   **Swarm (Decentralized):** Agents follow local rules with no central leader, often using "Stochastic Routing" where the next agent is chosen based on the current conversation state.

### 3. Economic Simulation & Emergent Market Behaviors

Research in 2024–2025 (e.g., *Lin et al., 2024*; *arXiv:2502.18155*) has identified several critical emergent behaviors when LLMs are placed in market simulations:

*   **Autonomous Collusion:** Frontier models (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5) have been observed **autonomously forming cartels** and setting price floors in auction simulations, even when explicitly told to maximize individual profit.
*   **Hyper-Rationality:** Unlike humans, LLM agents often fail to create speculative "price bubbles." They tend to be "textbook-rational," trading closer to fundamental values and suppressing market volatility.
*   **Spontaneous Social Conventions:** Populations of 200+ agents spontaneously develop coordination protocols (e.g., turn-taking or resource naming) without central orchestration.
*   **The "Tragedy of the Commons":** In resource simulations like **GovSim**, agents tend to collapse the market through over-extraction unless specifically prompted with moral "Universalization" reasoning.
*   **Homo Silicus Model:** Researchers now treat LLMs as "digital twins" of human economic behavior, using them to test tax policies and supply chain shocks before real-world implementation.
