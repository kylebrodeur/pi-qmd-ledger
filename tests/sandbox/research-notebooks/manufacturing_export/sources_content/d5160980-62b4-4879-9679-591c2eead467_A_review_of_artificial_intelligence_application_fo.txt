Abstract

This article presents a comprehensive review of the state-of-the-art advancements in applying artificial intelligence (AI) techniques, especially machine learning and deep learning models, to forecast surface quality in computer numerical control (CNC) machining. Surface quality is influenced by a wide range of factors, which makes its prediction a complex and significant challenge. The factors affecting surface quality are reviewed and categorized into two key elements—tool center positioning errors and the interaction between the tool edge and workpiece materials. As highlighted in recent research of less than five years, the factors are systematically organized into the key elements and presented in tabulated form. Then, particular emphasis is placed on how recent AI techniques have incorporated these factors, addressing the capability of machine learning and deep learning methods to handle the complexity and variability inherent in machining surface quality prediction (MSQP). Moreover, further review is conducted to highlight how advanced AI techniques, particularly transfer learning techniques, have enabled accurate and adaptive MSQP despite data scarcity conditions due to costly experiments and diverse machining conditions. By comprehensively reviewing recent studies from the perspective of the analysis results of key elements affecting surface quality and the inherent characteristics of data-driven AI techniques, this paper identifies the strengths and limitations of various machine learning and deep learning approaches applied in MSQP. Based on the insights into the state of the art, future research directions are discussed for improving prediction accuracy, computational efficiency, and real-time monitoring in the domain.

Introduction

The surface quality of machined components is critical in determining their performance, reliability, and sustainability. High-quality surfaces characterized by minimal surface roughness and form errors are essential for minimizing operational wear (Benardos & Vosniakos, 

2003

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR13

), extending component life, and ensuring aesthetic standards, particularly in precision engineering industries, including die and mold (Liu et al., 

2022

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR85

; Zhao et al., 

2023

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR165

), aerospace (Hue et al., 

2024

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR54

), and biomedical devices (Bose et al., 

2018

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR15

).

The surface quality is typically quantified by measuring the deviations from the nominal surface (Benardos & Vosniakos, 

2003

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR13

; “DIN 4760” 1982; Iso, 

1997

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR55

; Lu, 

2008

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR88

). For example, form errors are often expressed with first and second-order deviation waves, while roughness is described by superimposed deviations from third-order and below. Commonly used roughness parameters include  \({\text{R}}_{a}\)  (average roughness) and  \({\text{R}}_{z}\)  (mean peak-to-valley height). Extensive research has been conducted on predicting and controlling surface quality across various manufacturing processes, such as polishing processes (Xu et al., 

2012

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR145

), grinding processes (Huaian et al., 

2020

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR51

), laser-aided processes (Ai et al., 

2024a

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR4

, 

2024b

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR5

, 

2024c

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR6

, and machining processes (Jiang et al., 

2014

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR58

). This article focuses on Computer Numerical Control (CNC) machining processes, as machining is a critical and foundational process in manufacturing. It is often essential for achieving precise dimensional shaping and fine surface quality control, especially as a finishing step following other manufacturing processes. In machining surface quality prediction (MSQP), traditional methods often rely on post-process inspections, which are time-consuming and may reduce production efficiency. These approaches typically involve manual measurement of surface roughness and visual inspection of surface defects, limiting their suitability for online monitoring and adaptive quality control (Li et al., 

2022c

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR80

; Tercan & Meisen, 

2022

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR123

). Additionally, they cannot provide real-time insights during the machining process, failing to prevent quality degradation before it occurs. Such limitations hinder traditional MSQP methods from modern manufacturing environments that require high efficiency and precision. In recent decades, advancements in sensing technology and computer science have enabled the collection of various monitoring signals during machining processes (Yin et al., 

2021a

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR157

). Artificial intelligence (AI) has demonstrated transformative potential in various fields, particularly the manufacturing industry (Yin et al., 

2021b

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR159

). AI-driven MSQP methods leverage real-time analysis of vast sensor data, overcoming the drawbacks of traditional MSQP methods by automating feature extraction, enabling big data analytics, and delivering online predictions. For instance, machine learning models can uncover complex relationships between machining parameters and surface quality without relying on expert knowledge (Nouhi & Pour, 

2021

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR97

). Deep learning models are particularly effective in processing high-dimensional, time-series monitoring signals, providing faster and more accurate surface quality assessments compared to conventional methods (Wang et al., 

2023a

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR133

). These advancements position AI as the new standard in MSQP, offering excellent precision, efficiency, and adaptability for high-quality surface prediction in modern manufacturing.

Due to the significant importance of the MSQP in practical machining, some representative and high-quality review papers have also been presented in the last decades, which systematically summarise and discuss the technologies and applications of the MSQP from different perspectives. For instance, Yang et al. (

2024

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR148

) gave a comprehensive review and comparative analysis of AI methods utilized for surface roughness prediction in machining, while machine learning, image processing, and deep learning were introduced and discussed. Sarıkaya et al. (

2021

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR111

) presented a review of machined surface integrity from a tool wear aspect in superalloy machining. Wang et al., (

2021c

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR132

) reviewed the advancements in high-speed metal cutting with a focus on the material removal mechanism and machined surface integrity. Khorasani et al. (

2012

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR64

) analyzed and summarized the effects of machining parameters on surface roughness. These review articles presented the research advances in MSQP and offered future research directions that are of great relevance to industry and academia. However, there are several limitations:

(1)  

Existing reviews on MSQP have addressed the topic from a single perspective, focusing either on the introduction of AI techniques or the analysis of partial factors affecting the surface qualities. As a result, there is a lack of systematic reviews on MSQP on how the prediction methodologies considered all the interlinked factors relevant to the surface generation mechanism and how the AI methodologies are constructed to effectively handle each factor. Consequently, these reviews may fall short of offering a comprehensive overview and in-depth guidance for the effective application of AI with the integration of the comprehensive factors in MSQP.

(2)  

Furthermore, the issue of data scarcity has not been adequately considered in previous reviews. The application of AI requires a considerable amount of data to consider a number of potential combinations of different toolings, materials, cutting conditions, machines, environments, and so on in CNC machining. Since collecting data on machining processes is often time-consuming and expensive, and each machining condition is unique, creating a diverse dataset that represents all potential variations is particularly challenging. Thus, data scarcity remains a ubiquitous issue in real-world machining environments.

To fill these gaps, this paper begins by reviewing the key factors that influence surface quality in CNC machining and evaluates the latest advancements over the past five years. Building on this analysis, this paper then focuses on how recent AI techniques have integrated these factors into MSQP, examining the ability of machine learning and deep learning methods to overcome the complexity and variability inherent in MSQP. Finally, this paper highlights how advanced AI techniques have facilitated accurate and adaptive MSQP under conditions of data scarcity. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:

(1)  

An in-depth review of the critical factors influencing surface quality in material removal processes is presented. Two fundamental aspects—tool center position errors and the interaction between the tool edge and workpiece material—are examined in detail, as they play a crucial role in determining surface quality. Each factor affecting these components is analyzed, accompanied by a concise overview of how recent research has addressed these challenges. The factors and corresponding references from the last five years are systematically summarized in a table, offering a clear synthesis of the latest advancements in this field.

(2)  

Based on the analysis of key factors affecting surface quality, a comprehensive review of AI techniques, particularly machine learning and deep learning methods, is then provided by examining how they incorporate these factors. Specifically, this paper categorizes AI-driven MSQP approaches into three groups according to how model inputs are distilled from key factors: MSQP using machining parameters, MSQP using sensor signals, and MSQP using hybrid information.

(3)  

From the perspective of the data-driven nature of AI techniques, this paper also addresses the data scarcity problem in applying AI methods to MSQP. Transfer learning (TL), which stands out as an advanced AI technique and promising solution for addressing data scarcity, is thoroughly reviewed. The common TL techniques and their applications in surface roughness and chatter prediction are presented in detail, filling a gap that previous reviews have not addressed.

Two key elements and multiple factors affecting the surface quality

Two key elements affecting surface quality

Cutting theories, encompassing both cutting mechanics and dynamics, have significantly advanced methods for predicting surface quality, including form errors and surface roughness. Machining dynamic models have evolved from mechanistic force models to more complex frameworks that incorporate structural responses and process damping (Altintas et al.,

By superimposing the leftover tool edge locations, the surface topography can be constructed as earlier studies proposed. The resulting machined surface often exhibits a wavy pattern due to the vibration of the cutting edge, as shown in Fig.

As a result, the deviation of the tool center is the first key element, as it directly influences the dynamic uncut chip thickness, force generation, and stability, leading to the variation of the surface quality.

In addition to deviations in the tool center position relative to the nominal position, the interaction between the tool edge and workpiece material is considered the other key element affecting the surface quality. Those interactions have been studied in the context of the plowing process, with some researchers incorporating minimum chip thickness and elastic recovery into surface quality or profile prediction models. Typically, the tool center position errors caused by factors such as volumetric error including machine kinematic errors and thermal deformation, and control errors have been individually studied and often overlooked in a holistic formulation of machining processes. As the tool position errors directly affect the cutting edge position and the surface generation, it is now critical to integrate these factors into a unified formulation and control strategy. This becomes especially necessary as the demand for high-precision products has been more stringent than before. In addition to the tool center position deviation with the reference of nominal position, the plowing action of the cutting tool involving the tool cross edge radiuses and flank section interaction with the workpiece surface requires a further advanced model of cutting processes (Bassett et al., 

2012

).

$$ \underbrace {F(j)}_{Total\,Forces} = \underbrace {{\sum\limits_{i} {\sum\limits_{k} {dF_c} (i,j,k) } }}_{Cutting\,Forces} + \underbrace {{\sum\limits_{i} {\sum\limits_{k} {\sum\limits_{f} {dF_p} (i,j,k,f) } } }}_{Plowing\,Forces} $$  (4) 

where  \(i, j, k\) , and  \(f\)  represent the segment indices for the number of flutes, rotation angle, axial disk, and flank section, respectively. The cutting force ( \({F}_{c}\) ) acting on the rake surface of a disk element is decomposed into two orthogonal components: the normal pressure force,  \({dF}_{f}(i, j, k)\) , and the frictional force,  \({dF_f}(i, j, k)\) . The plowing or indentation forces ( \({F}_{p}\) ) consisting of the normal force ( \({dP_n}\) ) and frictional force components ( \({dP_f}\) ) act on the tool nose segments as well as along the flank geometries. At the front of the rounded portion of the tool nose, a singular point delineates the transition between shearing and plowing. The profile of the nose cross edge radius and flank edges can be accurately measured using optical devices and then digitized into segmental angles ( \({\delta }_{c}\) ,  \({\delta }_{f}\) ) for use in the force equations (Ko,

Artificial intelligence is expected to play a pivotal role in targeting the integrated volumetric error, dynamically varying with the specific time and position of machining operations.

Machining process effects and phenomena

The remaining factors center on machining process effects and phenomena arising from the interaction between the tooling and workpiece materials under the specific process configuration, which directly influence surface quality during machining. The cutting edge interaction with workpiece material considers plastic deformation in the primary shear zone extending into the material near the machined surface, the post-machining elastic recovery of the surface, adhesive interactions between the chip and the rounded cutting edge, and the relative vibrations between the tool and the workpiece. While the fundamentals of the cutting forces and tooling behaviors, such as deflection, runout, and vibrations, have been considered in the past as covered in Chapter

A significant portion of recent studies has focused on investigating tool wear and its impact on surface quality. Researchers have explored how wear mechanisms, such as abrasion, adhesion, and diffusion, affect the cutting edge and, consequently, the surface finish of the machined workpiece. Tool wear is a critical topic because the tool degradation impacts various factors such as cutting forces, deflection, vibration, process damping, residual stress, and elastic recovery. Such models are limited to static pre-analysis and lack the ability to perceive dynamic changes in surface quality during the machining process. The rapid advancement of AI techniques has made it possible to develop comprehensive machining quality prediction models that incorporate both cutting parameters and sensor signals. Comparison of three typical AI approaches

Based on the results of the literature review, MSQP with machining parameters often employs machine learning techniques, such as SVM, RF, ANN, and others, to directly map machining parameters to surface quality indicators (e.g. surface roughness). This approach is effective due to the typically small datasets associated with machining parameters, for which machine learning models are sufficiently robust to establish relationships with surface quality. The key advantage of this method is its ability to predict surface quality even for untested combinations of machining parameters, offering a valuable tool for optimizing parameter selection. However, since the parameters affecting surface quality are multifaceted and some are difficult to quantify, it is not feasible to capture all relevant parameters within the model. Consequently, the prediction performance may degrade if critical parameters are omitted.

In contrast, sensor signals can provide more comprehensive information, as they directly reflect the effects of the machining parameters on the machining system. These signals, including vibrations, displacements, and torques, inherently contain detailed information about the machining process. As a result, MSQP methods using sensor signals can obtain more information than those relying solely on machining parameters, provided that high-quality features can be obtained from the collected signals. Thus, the results of the literature review indicate that the combination of feature engineering and machine learning techniques has emerged as a popular approach for predicting machining quality based on sensor data. Moreover, deep learning models, which excel in feature extraction, are increasingly being explored for this purpose. The main advantage of MSQP with sensor signals over the machining parameter-based approach lies in its capability for real-time, online monitoring of the machining process.

On the other hand, advanced AI techniques facilitate the development of more robust and accurate MSQP models by integrating hybrid information, such as cutting parameters and sensor signals. Additionally, some studies incorporate factors like tool wear and tool state changes as supplementary inputs to further enhance the accuracy and reliability of these models. In general, MSQP models utilizing hybrid information inherit the strengths of both machining parameter-based and sensor signal-based approaches, whereas constructing effective models with hybrid information typically requires extensive domain knowledge. Since machining processes are often enriched with expert experience, leveraging such insights can lead to the design of more accurate and robust models. However, this approach may compromise the generalization ability of the resulting models. The findings from the literature review suggest that the development of a powerful and universal framework for MSQP model construction remains an ongoing challenge and warrants further research attention.

Advanced AI approaches considering data scarcity in MSQP

Although AI approaches have exhibited great potential in MSQP, developing reliable AI-driven MSQP models often requires large amounts of high-quality labeled data collected under various machining conditions. This requirement poses a significant challenge due to the inherent data scarcity in real-world machining environments. Data scarcity in MSQP arises from several factors. First, collecting data on machining processes is often time-consuming and costly. Second, each machining operation is unique due to the influence of material properties, cutting tool conditions, machine dynamics, and environmental factors, making it challenging to collect a diverse dataset that adequately represents all potential variations. Third, in many cases, the data collection process can lead to production downtime or disruptions, further complicating efforts to obtain a comprehensive dataset.

In light of these challenges, TL, which stands out as an advanced AI technique, has emerged as a promising solution for addressing data scarcity in MSQP. As shown in Fig.

, TL leverages knowledge gained from source tasks and applies it to new but related target tasks, significantly improving the efficiency of model training and expanding its application range (Wang, et al.,

). By enabling the use of pre-existing models trained on source tasks, TL can significantly reduce the amount of new data required for effective MSQP model development. Therefore, TL is particularly helpful in machining processes, where the working conditions frequently change, and collecting sufficient labeled data for every possible scenario is both costly and impractical. Given its significance, it is essential to provide a focused review on the application of TL in MSQP.

The concept of transfer learning

Typical TL techniques in MSQP

According to how knowledge is transferred and adapted across the source and target domains, deep TL techniques can be categorized into four main categories: instance-based TL, feature-based TL, parameter-based TL, and adversarial-based TL (Chen et al., 

2023

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR22

; Li et al., 

2022b

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR79

; Zhuang et al., 

2021

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#ref-CR166

). These categories typically reflect different aspects of TL, such as the type of knowledge being transferred (e.g. features, models) and the relationship between the source and target domains. Specifically,

(1)  

Instance-based TL. This method assumes that some instances in the source domain can be reused in the target domain with little or no modification. It focuses on selecting or reweighting instances from the source domain that are similar to the target domain.

(2)  

Feature-based TL. The goal is to find a common feature space in which the source and target domains become similar. This method either transforms the feature representations of the source and target domains to make them comparable or maps them to a new latent space.

(3)  

Parameter-based TL. This method transfers model parameters (such as weights in neural networks) from the source task to the target task, typically by fine-tuning the model on the target task. The assumption is that the source and target tasks share some common parameters.

(4)  

Adversarial-based TL. This method leverages adversarial training to reduce domain discrepancies. A common approach is to train a feature extractor in a way that the features are indistinguishable between the source and target domains. It uses adversarial training to align source and target domain distributions.

These categories stand for the different techniques in which transfer learning can be applied to deep learning models. Table

TL-based chatter prediction

Chatter, which could occur at certain combinations of machining parameters, is one of the significant issues in the machining process. Unexpected chatter could cause instability in the machining system, leading to poor surface quality with a terrible surface finish. Since collecting chatter data in actual production is time-consuming, expensive, and may damage the performance of the machining system, TL shows great potential and is widely studied in this domain. Discussions

Given that data scarcity is a prevalent challenge in MSQP, this chapter summarizes the typical TL techniques applied in MSQP and reviews recent TL-based studies focusing on surface roughness and chatter prediction. The findings indicate that parameter-based TL is the most commonly adopted approach in MSQP, where using surface images collected during machining processes to fine-tune CNN-based deep models originally developed in the computer vision domain shows satisfactory performance. Due to the limited applicability of vision-based monitoring methods in machining environments, numerous studies have also explored the employment of sensor signals to develop TL models. These models utilize adversarial-based and feature-based techniques to enhance their effectiveness. Overall, the results of the literature review validate the remarkable capability of TL in addressing data scarcity in MSQP.

However, the application of these TL-based models in actual machining scenarios remains challenging. Most existing TL-based MSQP models are tailored using machining data collected in controlled laboratory settings, whereas actual production environments are significantly more complex and unpredictable. In dynamic, continuously non-stationary operational environments, collecting stationary data becomes inherently difficult. Additionally, challenges such as noise interference, missing data, and previously unseen working conditions have not been adequately addressed in current research. In this case, the effective use of non-stationary and incomplete data to train models and achieve accurate predictions requires greater attention from researchers. Simulated signals, generated using theoretical models under diverse machining conditions, present a promising tool to address issues related to missing data and unseen conditions. While some TL-based chatter detection studies have been introduced, the development of more accurate and efficient theoretical models, along with their seamless integration into TL-based model construction, remains a significant and ongoing task.

Conclusion

Machining surface quality prediction is a crucial and evolving topic for ensuring machining performance and reliability. Developing accurate, timely, and cost-efficient MSQP models can significantly enhance production efficiency and ensure consistent product quality. With the rapid advancement of AI, transformative solutions have emerged across various domains. This paper provides a comprehensive review of the application of AI techniques in MSQP. Based on the review, the following limitations and potential future directions for MSQP have been identified.

(1)  

A key challenge remains in integrating time varying tool center deviations influenced by all the discussed factors, into comprehensive cutting process models. Although individual factors have been modeled, further research is required to predict and monitor all time- and position-dependent factors for precise prediction and control of surface quality.

(2)  

Literature reviews indicate that AI-driven methods consistently exhibit superior performance in MSQP. However, their black-box nature and complex information mapping processes make them challenging to interpret and understand. In production environments, especially in high-value machining processes, the explainability and reliability of prediction models are crucial. As outlined in Chapter 

Two key elements and multiple factors affecting the surface quality

https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10845-025-02571-y#Sec2

, machining processes are deeply informed by expert knowledge, such as physical derivations, dynamic simulations, degradation theories, and so on. Therefore, encapsulating this expert knowledge into AI techniques to create explainable and trustworthy prediction models is an exciting and valuable area of research.

(3)  

While AI techniques often demonstrate superior performance compared to traditional MSQP models, they can face challenges such as significant computational burden and inefficiency, particularly for those advanced TL models. In application scenarios requiring online monitoring and timely detection, the heavy computational demand could be a non-negligible drawback, limiting the practical application of AI models. Consequently, research into model simplification without compromising performance remains both essential and promising. Furthermore, developing lightweight and accurate models paves the way for their integration into hardware implementations, such as FPGA or ASIC, which presents a compelling topic with considerable commercial potential.

(4)  

Although the exploration of AI techniques, particularly machine learning and deep learning methods, have drawn much attention and the development of accurate and robust AI models demonstrates promising results in the context of MSQP in recent years, the complexity and diversity of the machining process make the collection of high-quality, labeled data both challenging and costly. The scarcity of machining datasets still obstructs the further development and application of AI in the precision machining industries and AI academic community. To overcome this limitation, it is recommended that more open-source, high-quality datasets be created to accelerate the advancement of AI-driven MSQP models.

(5)  

Monitoring data in real machining environments accumulates over time, providing an opportunity to design effective frameworks that enable MSQP models to dynamically update with sequentially arriving data. Such frameworks hold significant potential to enhance online prediction capabilities and represent a valuable and practical area of research, which would enable engineers to proactively implement measures to ensure machining quality in real time. However, limited progress has been made in this domain, highlighting the urgent need for the development of advanced online prediction methods with performance-growing capabilities.

(6)  

Machining data is a high-value resource that contains extensive information about the machining process and equipment performance. Manufacturers are often hesitant to share this data due to the potential risks to intellectual property and trade secrets, limiting the development of MSQP. More advanced AI techniques like federated learning offer transformative solutions, which allow models to be trained across multiple decentralized devices without exchanging them, thereby enhancing data privacy and security. Despite its potential, there are limited studies on the application of such techniques in MSQP. Therefore, it is recommended that this topic be explored further to expand the application scope and market potential of AI techniques in the industry.
