From economics of scale to economics of numbers with residents 

At higher policy levels and governments, where large-scale companies also sit at the table and lobby, people mainly think in large-scale systems. This can also more or less be found in the Reformers project, where particularly interesting techniques around hydrogen, flow batteries, etc. are discussed. There is nothing wrong with these systems, but some reflection on its time can do no harm. This way of thinking is inspired by the economies of scale known in microeconomics, which are apparently firmly incorporated into the DNA and all kinds of educational institutions. Schumacher's ‘Small is beautiful’ is sometimes professed with the mouth, but does not really get off the ground. 

In microeconomics, economies of scale is about the cost advantages that enterprises obtain due to their scale of operation. This is measured by the amount of output produced per unit of time of a production unit. This due to technical, organizational or related factors. In 1886, the first municipal energy company was founded and more municipalities followed, later merging into regional and provincial energy companies. A separate entity was founded for high voltage (now Tennet). Each production company remained responsible for power generation within its own operating area. 

The Electricity Act of 1989 focuses on the desire for economies of scale. A minimum installed capacity of 2,500 megawatts is  required for producers (Wikipedia).  The aim is to produce on a large scale as cheaply as possible, which is justifiable for certain old production systems. But the downside is that you incur more costs for distribution in investment and energy loss.  Before 1998, utility companies were allowed to own an electricity network and sell electricity simultaneously, which gave companies that owned the network unfair advantages over companies that were only active in retail sales of electricity. A new act demanded the decoupling of utilities and electricity supply. The generation and retail of electricity were liberalized, but transmission and distribution are still centralized with a monopoly position only for distribution. These distribution companies are not allowed to produce or store energy. So now production and distribution are separate and he energy in total is a less integrated system.  

The distribution companies may have a monopoly, but that position is limited, and they cannot use economies of scope formed by variety, and not volume as in economies of scale. Economies of scope is with a total cost of production decrease because of increasing the number of different goods produced. For example, a gas station primarily sells gasoline, but can sell soda, milk, baked goods, etc. and achieve economies of scope since with the same facility, each new product provides new added value for the customer who had otherwise spend his money elsewhere. So according to normal economic laws the energy distribution company would start offering services in the form of batteries because he is already at the customer's meter box. That means that he could have more added value for his customer who has too much energy from his PV panels. But in addition, he could also make agreements in such a way that the grid is less overloaded. Now, due to the limitation of the distributor function, the customer's money is spent on buying less sustainable energy while there is a chance that the supply of the PV panels is stopped due to overloading of the grid. In addition to the fact that there are nearly no subsidies in the Netherlands for batteries, there are also institutional obstacles for an optimal energy grid with the use of batteries.

In the meantime, new technologies are developing fast in the international market that make decentralized energy systems cheaper on a small scale and in a sustainable way.  PV panels are becoming increasingly efficient and supplying more and more energy, but at times when demand is low, which problem can be solved with batteries instead of cables. Cables transport energy to other places where you may also have a surplus of energy or panels are switched of.  Later environmentally unfriendly production techniques requiring fuel are used when energy is needed. Then still investments in unsustainable techniques are necessary, where you can do better with batteries. What you want is to store the energy for times when there is no sun.  

Is that an option? Technically, that is no problem at all. For example, if all houses in Heiloo were equipped with solar panels with a 10 kWh battery, there would be little chance of grid congestion for that area. The condition is of course that residents participate and buy batteries. But they are waiting for a lower price. And the price for batteries go down if production reaches a certain scale due to demand from residents (a chicken and egg problem). For the economy of scale (for companies), you need an economy of numbers in a decentralized system.  That makes a decentralized efficient energy production system possible. More solar energy with batteries provides a more sustainable, stable and efficient energy system. With enough batteries there are no transportation losses in low voltage grids (high losses) and grids on higher level. A system with decentralized batteries (home and neighborhood) is more reliable because it is not like cables where an entire neighborhood is without power in the event of a fault. Batteries can be made in mass production (low cost at high volume) where cable with groundwork and distribution stations are more labor-intensive. So there seem to be advantages, especially if there is congestion on the electricity grid. Unfortunately, there is little guidance from the governments for that scenario.  

In summary, an institutional system has emerged in which distribution companies cannot apply economics of scope and in which the government provides little support for battery applications, with the result that turnover and knowledge development are insufficient for decentralised distribution systems for economics of scale, which means that the price remains high and economics of numbers remain insufficient to achieve an efficient energy system in the Netherlands. A few voices crying in the wilderness like the author of this blog will probably not change that in the short term. But in any case, I am happy that I can make my voice heard within Reformers in a professional team.
