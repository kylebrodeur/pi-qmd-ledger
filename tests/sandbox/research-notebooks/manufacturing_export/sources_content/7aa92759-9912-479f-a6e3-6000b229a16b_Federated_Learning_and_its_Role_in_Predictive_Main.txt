Abstract

Predictive maintenance (PdM) has emerged as a cornerstone of Industry 4.0, enabling organizations to anticipate equipment failures, optimize maintenance schedules, and reduce operational costs. The convergence of artificial intelligence (AI), the Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT), and advanced analytics has facilitated the transition from reactive and preventive maintenance to predictive and even prescriptive strategies. Within this context, federated learning (FL) offers a transformative approach to PdM by enabling collaborative model training across distributed and privacy-sensitive data sources without the need to centralize raw data. This chapter explores the integration of FL into PdM systems, highlighting its potential to improve sustainability and resilience in industrial maintenance. The discussion extends beyond technical mechanisms to include enabling technologies such as edge and fog computing, IIoT platforms, and blockchain. Furthermore, it addresses the sociotechnical implications of privacy-preserving AI in industrial environments, focusing on its role in supporting worker expertise and ensuring sustainable practices. Case studies, key challenges, and future research directions are presented to provide a comprehensive view of the field.

1. Introduction

The Fourth Industrial Revolution, or Industry 4.0, marks a fundamental transformation in the way industrial systems are designed, operated, and maintained. Driven by the convergence of Industrial Internet of Things (IIoT), cyber-physical systems, and artificial intelligence (AI), Industry 4.0 promotes the creation of interconnected and intelligent manufacturing environments that can adapt dynamically to changing conditions. In this paradigm, data are generated continuously by machines, sensors, and control systems, enabling advanced analytics to inform operational decisions in near real-time.

Among the many applications of AI in Industry 4.0, predictive maintenance (PdM) stands out as a strategic capability for ensuring asset reliability, reducing downtime, and optimizing resource usage [ 

1

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B1

]. PdM systems analyze patterns in operational data to forecast potential equipment failures before they occur, allowing maintenance to be scheduled proactively rather than reactively. This shift can lead to substantial cost savings, improved safety, and extended equipment lifespans [ 

2

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B2

].

Traditional PdM approaches typically rely on centralized data collection, where information from multiple assets and sites is aggregated in a central repository for model training. While effective in theory, this approach faces practical limitations in industrial contexts. Sensitive operational data may be subject to confidentiality agreements, regulatory constraints, or competitive concerns, making organizations reluctant to share it. Furthermore, the heterogeneity of equipment, operating environments, and data formats can complicate the integration process. Bandwidth and latency constraints in remote or resource-limited environments also pose significant challenges for transmitting large volumes of high-frequency sensor data [ 

3

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B3

].

Federated learning (FL) offers a paradigm shift in how predictive models can be trained in such contexts. Instead of moving raw data to a central location, FL allows multiple entities – factories, industrial sites, or even individual machines – to train models locally and share only the learned parameters or gradients. This preserves data privacy, reduces the need for high-bandwidth connections, and enables the exploitation of diverse datasets without compromising ownership or confidentiality [ 

4

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B4

].

Beyond these technical benefits, FL aligns well with broader goals of sustainability and human-centered digitalization. By optimizing maintenance interventions, FL-based PdM can minimize unnecessary component replacements, reduce material waste, and lower energy consumption. Additionally, by ensuring that AI-driven recommendations remain transparent and interpretable, FL can support maintenance personnel in combining operational expertise with algorithmic outputs.

2. Predictive maintenance in Industry 4.0

Predictive maintenance represents a data-driven evolution of traditional maintenance practices, shifting the focus from responding to failures after they occur or following fixed preventive schedules to anticipating potential issues before they manifest. This predictive capability is enabled by continuous monitoring of equipment through sensors and control systems, coupled with advanced analytics techniques that detect patterns indicative of wear, degradation, or abnormal behavior [ 

5

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B5

].

In industrial environments, PdM offers significant operational advantages. By identifying early signs of failure, it reduces unplanned downtime, which can be extremely costly in high-throughput manufacturing or critical infrastructure [ 

6

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B6

]. Optimized maintenance schedules also reduce labor costs and spare parts usage, leading to a more efficient allocation of resources. Furthermore, improved equipment reliability enhances worker safety, as failures are prevented before they can cause hazardous situations. The sustainability dimension is equally important: Fewer unnecessary replacements translate into reduced waste, lower raw material consumption, and decreased environmental impact [ 

7

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B7

].

PdM systems draw on a variety of data sources. Vibration analysis, acoustic monitoring, thermal imaging, and oil analysis are among the most common condition-monitoring techniques. IIoT-enabled devices provide real-time telemetry from distributed assets, while enterprise resource planning (ERP) systems and maintenance management software supply historical data on repairs and operational performance. Integrating these diverse datasets, however, is not without challenges. Variations in data formats, sampling rates, and sensor accuracy can complicate analysis. Moreover, the availability of labeled failure data is often limited, as failures may be rare events in well-maintained systems. Finally, concerns about the security and privacy of operational data – especially in multiorganization collaborations – can restrict the scope of data sharing, potentially limiting model accuracy [ 

6

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B6

, 

7

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B7

].

3. Federated learning fundamentals

Federated learning is a decentralized machine learning paradigm designed to enable collaborative model training without the need to share raw data [ 

4

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B4

]. In its most common form, FL involves a set of participating clients – such as industrial sites, edge devices, or even individual machines – that each hold their own local datasets. Instead of transmitting this data to a central server, each client trains a local model and sends only the resulting updates (parameters or gradients) to a coordinating server, often referred to as the aggregator.

The aggregator then combines these updates, typically using algorithms such as federated averaging (FedAvg), to produce a new global model that incorporates knowledge from all participating clients. This updated global model is then redistributed to the clients, and the process repeats until the model converges or a specified performance threshold is achieved.

The architecture of FL can take different forms depending on the application and infrastructure. In a centralized FL setting, a single aggregator coordinates all communication between clients. In a decentralized or peer-to-peer approach, clients share updates directly with each other, which can enhance robustness but requires more complex coordination. Hierarchical FL architectures, in which local aggregators collect updates from nearby clients before forwarding them to a central server, can further reduce communication costs and improve scalability.

For industrial applications, FL offers several practical advantages. Privacy is preserved because raw data never leave the local site, addressing both regulatory compliance and competitive concerns. Communication efficiency is improved because only model updates, which are typically much smaller than raw datasets, are transmitted. Moreover, FL can leverage the diversity of data from multiple sites to improve the generalization capability of predictive models, making them more robust to varying operating conditions.

Nevertheless, FL also presents challenges that must be addressed for effective deployment. Statistical heterogeneity – where the data distributions across clients differ significantly – can slow convergence and reduce model accuracy. Communication overhead, while lower than raw data transfer, can still be significant in environments with limited bandwidth. Finally, FL is not immune to security risks; malicious clients can attempt to poison models, and adversaries may infer information about local datasets from shared updates. Ongoing research in secure aggregation, differential privacy, and blockchain-based model provenance aims to address these issues [ 

8

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B8

– 

10

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B10

].

4. Integrating FL into PdM workflows

The convergence of FL with PdM workflows is reshaping how industries manage the health and performance of their assets. Traditionally, PdM systems begin by collecting sensor data from machines, such as vibration, temperature, or pressure, through IIoT devices. These data are often processed locally, either on embedded controllers or edge computing nodes, where basic steps such as filtering and feature extraction are performed [ 

11

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B11

].

In centralized architectures, these features are typically sent to the cloud for model training. However, FL offers a decentralized alternative. Each device or facility trains its own local model using private data, such as usage logs, maintenance history, and sensor signals, without exposing sensitive information. After several rounds of local training, only the model parameters – not the raw data – are shared with a central server. The server aggregates them to update a global model, which is then redistributed for further local refinement. This enables iterative model updates while ensuring data remain private at each site [ 

12

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B12

].

This setup is especially effective in cases where different companies or factories want to collaborate without sharing proprietary data. For example, several manufacturing plants using similar equipment can jointly develop predictive models while keeping internal data secure. FL enables this by sharing only model updates, which, under appropriate safeguards, cannot be reverse-engineered to reveal private information [ 

13

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B13

].

5. Enabling technologies

The successful deployment of FL-based PdM systems relies on a robust ecosystem of enabling technologies that support local computation, secure communication, and scalable collaboration across distributed industrial settings. These technologies work in concert to ensure that FL can be practically and securely applied in real-world industrial environments.

5.1 Edge and fog computing

Edge computing plays a central role by bringing processing capabilities closer to the data source. In the context of PdM, this allows for real-time analysis of sensor signals – such as vibration, thermal, or current measurements – directly on or near the machine. This proximity reduces both communication latency and bandwidth consumption while enabling timely fault detection and response [ 

16

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B16

]. Fog computing extends this approach by creating an intermediate processing layer between edge devices and the cloud. Fog nodes can locally aggregate model updates in an FL setting, filter noisy inputs, or pretrain partial models before uploading to a central server, thereby improving scalability and reducing communication bottlenecks.

5.2 Industrial IoT platforms

Industrial IoT platforms provide the infrastructure required for device integration, data acquisition, and secure communication in distributed industrial ecosystems. Protocols such as MQTT and standards such as OPC UA ensure interoperability across heterogeneous assets. In an FL-enabled PdM workflow, IIoT platforms manage not only the real-time flow of operational data but also the orchestration of local training and the secure transmission of model parameters to aggregation points. These platforms are critical to ensuring that devices with diverse manufacturers and capabilities can participate uniformly in federated training cycles.

5.3 Blockchain and secure aggregation

As FL involves multiple parties contributing to a shared model, establishing trust becomes essential. Blockchain offers a decentralized ledger for tracking model updates, ensuring that training contributions are recorded immutably and transparently. This is particularly beneficial in collaborative PdM scenarios across supply chains or consortia, where auditability and traceability of model evolution are critical. When combined with secure aggregation techniques, such as homomorphic encryption or differential privacy, blockchain also protects model integrity while safeguarding sensitive operational data from exposure or manipulation [ 

16

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B16

].

5.4 Digital twins

Digital twins are digital replicas of physical assets or systems that can mirror real-time behavior through sensor data streams. When integrated with FL-trained PdM models, digital twins provide a powerful tool for scenario simulation, proactive diagnostics, and maintenance optimization. For instance, FL models can be validated virtually on a digital twin before deployment, reducing risk and accelerating rollout. Digital twins can also generate synthetic scenarios that improve FL model personalization, while model updates enhance the fidelity of the twin.

5.5 Hybrid FL and label synchronization

An emerging line of research also highlights the importance of hybrid FL approaches and techniques such as label synchronization. While classical FL assumes independent and identically distributed (IID) data across clients, industrial environments often present highly heterogeneous (non-IID) datasets due to differences in equipment, operating conditions, and data labeling practices. Hybrid FL schemes combine global model aggregation with localized personalization, while label synchronization ensures consistency in data representation across sites. These advances are particularly relevant in smart manufacturing contexts, where federated models must generalize across diverse production lines while preserving privacy [ 

17

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B17

].

6. Sustainability and human-centered perspectives

While the technical merits of FL in PdM are evident, its broader contributions to sustainability and human-centered design are equally significant. By enabling more accurate maintenance scheduling, FL allows organizations to replace components only when necessary. This leads to reduced material waste, lower energy consumption, and a smaller carbon footprint. In sectors such as aerospace, even marginal improvements in predicting component life can result in substantial resource savings and emission reductions [ 

18

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B18

].

The decentralized nature of FL also supports environmental goals by distributing computational workloads across existing local infrastructure. This reduces reliance on energy-intensive centralized data centers. When paired with energy-aware model training techniques, FL becomes a component of more sustainable AI practices [ 

19

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B19

].

From a human-centered perspective, FL-based PdM systems are designed to support and enhance the expertise of maintenance personnel – not replace them. By delivering interpretable and context-aware predictions, FL-based systems empower technicians to combine algorithmic insights with experiential knowledge [ 

7

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B7

].

Interpretability vignette:

 Consider a technician receiving an FL-based alert: “High probability of gearbox anomaly (87%). Contributing features: vibration amplitude (+0.32), temperature rise (+0.28). Recommended action: inspect lubrication system.” Such explanations support maintenance personnel by bridging AI outputs with human expertise, increasing trust and effective decision-making.

Moreover, FL's privacy-preserving architecture ensures that sensitive operational data remain within the organization, addressing concerns over surveillance, data misuse, or loss of control in collaborative environments. Sociotechnical challenges also emerge as workers interact with increasingly intelligent systems. These include the need for new skillsets, retraining, and mechanisms to interpret predictive outputs. As a result, successful deployment of FL in PdM requires structured training programs and transparent communication about how models are developed, updated, and validated. Transparency about model development and validation reduces concerns about automation and emphasizes AI as a support tool [ 

20

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B20

].

7.1 Wind energy sector

Wind turbines are often spread across large areas, generating data in heterogeneous environmental conditions. A real-world deployment by Li et al. [ 

21

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B21

] introduced a FL-based SCADA data model to monitor wind turbines without sharing raw data. Their decentralized framework enabled operators across geographically dispersed farms to collaboratively improve fault detection accuracy for gearbox and rotor anomalies. The model preserved SCADA data privacy and reduced communication overhead. Similarly, Arooj et al. [ 

22

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B22

] proposed FedWindT, a transformer-based FL model that significantly enhanced predictive accuracy across diverse wind conditions and topographies, illustrating the robustness of collaborative FL in wind power forecasting and maintenance planning.

7.2 Railway infrastructure

Railway infrastructure requires continuous monitoring to preempt failures in signaling systems, track integrity, and rolling stock. A study by Zhang et al. [ 

23

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B23

] developed a FL system for railway point machines, enabling fault diagnosis without requiring central data consolidation. This asynchronous FL system addressed class imbalance and imperfect data transmission across railway nodes. It allowed regional operators to build models using vibration and event-log data locally, improving system uptime and reducing unplanned repairs. The result was a more secure, privacy-preserving diagnostic system suitable for cross-border railway operators.

7.3 Smart manufacturing

In manufacturing, PdM plays a central role in reducing downtime and ensuring high production quality. Rosero et al. (2024) [ 

17

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B17

] proposed a hybrid federated learning framework with label synchronization to address the challenge of non-IID data across distributed manufacturing sites. The approach was validated in real-world scenarios, including automated product inspection in Bosch factories and RUL prediction for aircraft components. Results showed that the proposed hybrid FL method achieved performance comparable to centralized models while preserving data privacy and reducing the need for raw data sharing. By improving model generalization in heterogeneous environments, this work demonstrates how FL can enhance PdM in smart manufacturing ecosystems.

7.4 Energy sector and power grids

FL has proven valuable in monitoring high-voltage transformers, circuit breakers, and substations. In one initiative detailed by Sundsgaard (2025) [ 

24

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B24

], medium-voltage cable networks were analyzed using distributed machine learning frameworks, including FL, to detect partial discharges and insulation faults. By combining data from multiple utilities operating under different grid conditions, the federated models achieved higher reliability in fault prediction while meeting regional data protection requirements.

Hassan (2025) [ 

25

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B25

] applied FL to the monitoring of offshore wind farms, focusing on condition monitoring and PdM of turbines. The approach improved the detection of failures in critical components such as gearboxes and blades while ensuring data remained locally stored at each wind farm. To strengthen trust between operators, blockchain was used to guarantee the integrity of model updates, providing traceability and auditability in collaborative learning.

7.5 Maritime industry

Although outside traditional land-based industries, the maritime sector also showcases FL adoption. Kalafatelis et al. [ 

26

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B26

] surveyed FL-enabled predictive models in shipping fleets to anticipate failures in propulsion and auxiliary systems. The distributed learning scheme protected sensitive operational data from vessels registered under different jurisdictions, demonstrating the feasibility of FL across maritime fleets under different jurisdictions.

These cases illustrate that FL can be applied across sectors such as wind energy, railways, manufacturing, and shipping, while addressing privacy and data-sharing constraints.

8. Benefits, challenges, and open issues

FL provides multiple benefits for PdM applications. By enabling collaborative model training without sharing raw data, it preserves privacy, fosters interorganizational cooperation, and reduces communication overhead compared to traditional centralized approaches. The ability to leverage diverse datasets enhances model robustness and prediction reliability across heterogeneous environments. Additionally, FL inherently supports sustainability by optimizing maintenance interventions, which reduce unnecessary component replacements and minimize environmental impact.

Figure 3

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#F3

 summarizes the main benefits and challenges of applying FL to PdM, providing a visual complement to the discussion in this section.

 

Figure 3.

Benefits and challenges of FL in PdM. The left column highlights privacy, scalability, robustness, reduced communication, and sustainability. The right column lists key challenges: data heterogeneity, communication cost, security risks, governance/trust, and regulatory compliance.

Nevertheless, significant challenges remain. Statistical heterogeneity in client data can hinder convergence and degrade model accuracy, requiring the development of adaptive aggregation algorithms and personalization strategies. Communication overhead, although lower than raw data transfer, can still be substantial in bandwidth-constrained environments, especially for large models. Security threats, including model poisoning and inference attacks, remain active areas of concern, necessitating the integration of secure aggregation protocols, differential privacy techniques, and robust anomaly detection for updates. Operational deployment must also address model drift and client drop-out. Standard mitigation strategies include periodic retraining of the global model and the use of robust aggregation methods to ensure model stability despite fluctuating client participation.

From an organizational perspective, the successful adoption of FL in PdM requires trust among participants, clear governance frameworks for model ownership, and well-defined procedures for handling disputes or inconsistencies in contributions. Additionally, regulatory compliance – particularly with respect to data protection laws such as the GDPR – must be carefully managed, even if raw data are never exchanged.

In multiparty FL consortia, the global model is typically coowned under agreed service-level terms, while client contributions may be weighted according to data volume or quality. Audit logs ensure accountability, and disputes are usually managed contractually, aligning with GDPR and sector-specific governance practices.

Open research issues include the standardization of FL protocols for industrial applications, the development of lightweight models suitable for deployment on resource-constrained edge devices, and the integration of explainability mechanisms that can provide maintenance personnel with transparent and actionable insights.

9. Future directions

The integration of FL into PdM will advance with progress in AI, edge computing, and industrial networking. One promising direction is the combination of FL with TinyML, enabling the deployment of ultra-lightweight predictive models directly on microcontrollers embedded in machinery. This approach could bring predictive capabilities to even the most resource-constrained assets, further decentralizing intelligence in industrial systems.

Another important direction is the development of hybrid FL schemes that combine global model aggregation with site-specific personalization. Such approaches allow predictive models to adapt to local operating conditions while still benefiting from collaborative knowledge sharing. Recent work has also highlighted the use of label synchronization to address the challenge of non-IID data in industrial environments, ensuring that heterogeneous datasets remain compatible during training and improving model generalization without requiring centralized data consolidation [ 

17

https://www.intechopen.com/online-first/1233887#B17

]. These techniques are particularly relevant in manufacturing ecosystems, where variations in equipment and production lines often lead to significant data heterogeneity.

Security will remain a priority, with increasing emphasis on quantum-safe cryptographic techniques to protect FL communications against future quantum computing threats. Moreover, the integration of FL with self-healing networks and autonomous maintenance systems may enable more automated and adaptive PdM solutions.

The role of FL in supporting sustainability will also expand. By incorporating environmental impact metrics into predictive models, maintenance decisions can be optimized not only for cost and reliability but also for ecological footprint, enabling strategies that balance operational efficiency with environmental goals.

10. Conclusion

Federated learning represents a paradigm shift in how PdM models are developed and deployed in Industry 4.0. By enabling collaborative training across distributed datasets while preserving privacy, FL addresses key limitations of traditional centralized approaches. Its use in PdM improves prediction accuracy, supports optimized maintenance scheduling, and contributes to sustainability by reducing waste and energy consumption.

The success of FL, however, depends on more than technical advances. Organizational trust, regulatory compliance, workforce training, and human-centered design will be essential for widespread adoption and long-term effectiveness. As industrial systems evolve toward greater autonomy and interconnectivity, FL is poised to become a cornerstone of PdM, helping industries shift from reactive repairs to proactive, data-driven maintenance strategies.
