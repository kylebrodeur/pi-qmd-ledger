W orld Models in AI: A Comprehensive

Overview

Introduction

Large-scale world models in AI refer to advanced foundation models or AI systems capable of

simulating and predicting complex aspects of the real world. These models are trained on vast,

multimodal datasets (text, images, audio, video, sensor data, etc.) and aim to internalize the

dynamics of the physical world.

Unlike traditional AI focused on narrow tasks, world models learn rich representations of

environments – enabling spatial reasoning, temporal predictions, and causal understanding

beyond text alone research.aimultiple.com arionresearch.com. In essence, a world model serves

as an AI's “mental model” of reality, allowing it to imagine scenarios, forecast outcomes, and

plan actions before executing them arionresearch.com arionresearch.com. This capability marks

a shift from reactive AI (responding to inputs with learned patterns) to AI with foresight and

reasoning, bridging the gap between purely digital intelligence and real-world autonomous

behavior. World models are seen as a critical stepping stone toward more general AI, because

they integrate knowledge across domains and modalities to behave in more human-

like, context-aware ways.

Why World Models Matter: By modeling real-world complexity, these systems unlock new

possibilities that were hard to achieve with traditional approaches. They can maintain long-term

coherence in interactive settings, predict the effects of actions (“what if I do X?”), and generat e

realistic simulations (e.g. lengthy videos or 3D environments) that obey physical laws

research.aimultiple.com research.aimultiple.com.

This makes them invaluable for domains like robotics, autonomous vehicles, climate science,

and any AI application requiring an understanding of space, physics, and cause-effect beyond

static data. For instance, a large world model can watch a video and answer questions about

what will happen next, or imagine how a scene might evolve over time – something a text-only

language model cannot do research.aimultiple.com research.aimultiple.com.

In summary, world models represent a convergence of scale (large models), scope (multiple

modalities), and simulation ability, aiming to ground AI in reality and enable more advanced

reasoning and planning.

Key Organizations Developing and Deploying World Models

. Below we highlight key players and their contributions:

•

OpenAI (USA): OpenAI has driven the paradigm of large foundation models with

systems like GPT-4 (a large language model) and DALL-E 3 (image generation). While

primarily language or image-focused, these models are stepping stones toward world

models. OpenAI's roadmap explicitly aims for Artificial General Intelligence (AGI),

and integrating world knowledge is central to that. OpenAI is also exploring robotics and

agents – for example, investing in robotics startups (e.g. 1X Technologies for humanoid

robots) – hinting at plans to eventually embed language models into physical world

contexts. OpenAI's models are deployed via Microsoft's Azure cloud and power

numerous applications, illustrating the commercial deployment of world-model-like AI

in everything from coding assistants to chatbots.

•

Google DeepMind (USA/UK): Formed by merging Google Brain and DeepMind, this

lab is at the forefront of world model research. Google DeepMind's Genie project is a

prime example – Genie 2 is described as a “large-scale foundation world model” that

generates diverse interactive 3D environments from a single image prompt

research.aimultiple.com. Genie 2 can simulate physics (gravity, water, lighting) and

allow agents or humans to act within the generated world research.aimultiple.com.

DeepMind has also produced world-model-based reinforcement learning advances:

the Dreamer series (V2, V3) of agents use latent world models to outperform traditional

model-free RL algorithms arionresearch.com. In science,

DeepMind's AlphaFold leveraged massive genomic data to understand the “world” of

protein folding, dramatically outperforming decades-old methods by predicting structures

with atomic accuracy nature.com nature.com. Going forward, Google DeepMind is

reportedly working on a next-generation model called Gemini, which will combine

techniques from AlphaGo (planning and game-play) with language and vision –

essentially an AGI-oriented world model meant to rival or surpass GPT-4. DeepMind's

work exemplifies how a lab can integrate simulation, game environments, and real-

world data to build powerful world models.

•

NVIDIA (USA): Known for hardware, NVIDIA is also developing AI models and

platforms. Notably, NVIDIA's Cosmos platform provides open world foundation models

(WFMs) for “physical AI” nvidia.com. Cosmos includes models like Cosmos

Predict (which can generate 30-second video forecasts of future world states from sensor

inputs), Cosmos Transfer (for sim- to-real domain transfer in environments), and Cosmos

Reason (a vision-language model for physical reasoning) nvidia.com nvidia.com. These

models help robots, autonomous vehicles, and industrial systems simulate and reason

about their environments. NVIDIA's strategy is to offer these pre-trained world models

(available under open licenses) to developers to accelerate robotics, AV, and digital twin

simulations nvidia.com nvidia.com. For example, Autonomous vehicle training can use

Cosmos to generate diverse sensor data (different weather, lighting, city layouts) in

3

simulation, saving time and cost in preparing AVs for rare scenarios nvidia.com

nvidia.com. NVIDIA is thus a key enabler, providing both the compute

infrastructure (GPUs like the new Blackwell series nvidia.com) and the world models

needed to build complex AI systems.

•

Meta (Facebook) AI (USA): Meta's AI research (formerly FAIR) has been heavily

involved in large models, though with a slightly different philosophy. In 2023, Meta

released LLaMA 2, a large language model open-sourced for research and commercial

use. While not a full world model (it's text-based), Meta's open approach sparked a wave

of innovation as developers fine-tuned LLaMA for various tasks and modalities. Meta is

also researching embodied AI – e.g. the Habitat simulator for training household robots,

an d multi-sensor robots that learn world models of their environment. Meta's Chief AI

Scientist, Yann LeCun, has argued that true AI needs world models and sensory

grounding beyond language. In fact, in late 2025 LeCun announced he is leaving Meta to

start a new venture focused explicitly on world models and AI that can understand and

interact with the physical world, indicating a belief that Meta's current direction (largely

LLM-centric) isn't sufficient finance.coin-turk.com finance.coin-turk.com. Still, Meta's

corporate roadmap integrates AI into its products (Instagram, WhatsApp,

VR/AR metaverse applications). We see Meta deploying generative AI chatbots and

image/video generators for consumers, and it's likely they will incorporate multimodal

world modeling (for example, AI that can see through AR glasses and converse about

one's surroundings). Meta's investment in spatial computing (AR/VR) aligns with

world models that can populate interactive virtual worlds – a concept akin to an AI-

driven “metaverse”.

•

Microsoft (USA): Microsoft has partnered with OpenAI and is integrating world-model-

like AI across its software ecosystem. Its Copilot products (for coding, Office

documents, etc.) are powered by foundation models that understand user context

(documents, emails, codebase) and act as knowledgeable assistants. Microsoft's Azure AI

services also offer foundation models (like the OpenAI APIs and its own Vision and

Language models) to enterprise customers. Strategically, Microsoft is investing in cloud

infrastructure at massive scale (committing $80B on AI data centers in 2025 alone) to

support these large model s atlanticcouncil.org. While Microsoft's R&D on novel models

is often through OpenAI or internal labs (e.g. it built the “Turing” series of large

language models), its key role is deploying and commercializing world models

globally. Microsoft's backing of OpenAI (and more recently, an investment stake in

Meta's open AI efforts) shows its intent to remain at the forefront of AI capabilities via

corporate alliances. In summary, Microsoft is a major facilitator and adopter of world

models, embedding them as foundational tech across cloud and productivity tools.

•

Anthropic (USA): A startup founded by ex-OpenAI researchers, Anthropic is focused on

large language models with an emphasis on safety and alignment. Its model Claude is a

100B+ parameter LLM that can be seen as a world model of language and knowledge.

Anthropic's roadmap envisions ever larger “Claude-next” models (they have discussed

aiming for 10× the power of today's models in the next 2-3 years) and novel training

techniques (like “Constitutional AI” for safe behavior). While Anthropic's models are

currently text-based, the company's mission is to steer the development of general

AI cautiously, which likely means extending into models that understand the world more

deeply. Notably, Anthropic just secured a major investment from Amazon, which will

4

integrate Claude into AWS. This indicates startups are partnering with tech giants to

obtain the compute resources for building frontier world models. Anthropic's trajectory

highlights that safety-conscious development of world models is a priority for some

organizations, given the strategic importance of these models and their broad societal

impact.

•

Chinese Tech Labs (China): China's tech giants and research institutes are racing to

build their own foundation/world models. Baidu released ERNIE Bot, a large language

and multimodal model (comparable to GPT-4) intended to power search and productivity

in Chinese. Alibaba has Tongyi Qianwen for enterprise AI services,

and Tencent and Huawei have launched large models (Huawei's Pangu models include

a weather prediction model that outperforms traditional numerical weather methods

huaweicloud.com). The Beijing Academy of AI (BAAI) created WuDao 2.0, one of the

largest models (1.75 trillion parameters) across images and text, as a national research

effort. These organizations often collaborate with government in a state-backed push to

achieve AI parity with the West. Chinese world models are being deployed in everything

from social media and finance to smart city systems. While some of these models are

slightly behind the very cutting-edge (e.g., GPT-4), China is rapidly improving – and

its national strategy explicitly calls for AI leadership by 2030, fueling heavy investment

(see Section on national strategies). We are also seeing Chinese labs focus on open-

source releases (as a means to gain academic influence) as well as specialized models

(like medicine, engineering) integrated into industry. In summary, China's key

organizations (Baidu, Alibaba, Tencent, Huawei, BAAI, and many startups) view world

models as critical strategic technology and are advancing them under government

guidance.

•

Notable Startups & Labs: A number of smaller organizations are innovating in world

models. For example, Decart (USA) has an Oasis platform that lets users generate and

explore adaptive virtual worlds with real-time interactions – akin to a generative

Minecraft-like universe research.aimultiple.com. Decart also works on GPU

optimization for training large world models, aiming to make deployment more efficient

research.aimultiple.com. World Labs (USA), co-founded by leading AI professor Fei-

Fei Li, is focused on spatial intelligence – their tech can take a single 2D image and turn

it into an interactive 3D environment with depth and physics research.aimultiple.com.

This startup quickly reached “unicorn” status, showing investor excitement for world

models that enable easy creation of virtual worlds. Another example, Jua (Switzerland),

is building a foundation world model for weather and earth simulation – trained on

petabytes of sensor data to simulate global climate patterns (a potentially transformative

tool for forecasting and climate science) jua.ai. We also have research labs like

Stanford's Center for Research on Foundation Models (which studies and releases

models like BioLang for biology, etc.), and nonprofits like EleutherAI and LAION in

the West or BAAI in China that release open models and datasets. These smaller players

often tackle specific domains or push transparency, complementing the work of the tech

giants.

Overall, an ecosystem of players is propelling world models forward: the US leads in sheer

number of large models (in 2025, U.S. organizations produced ~40 large foundation models, vs.

~15 in China and only ~3 in Europe atlanticcouncil.org), thanks to companies like OpenAI,

5

Google, Meta, and vibrant startups. China's tech firms and institutes are catching up fast with

strong state support. Europe, while home to top AI researchers, lacks industry-scale models (due

to limited consolidated funding and compute), but initiatives are underway to boost capacity (e.g.

EU's planned “AI lighthouse” compute centers). This competitive landscape underlines that

world models are not just scientific projects – they are strategic assets for companies and

countries alike.

World Models vs Traditional AI: Technical, Economic, and

Strategic Comparison

World models differ from traditional AI approaches in fundamental ways. Below we compare

them across technical, economic, and strategic dimensions:

Technical Differences

Scope and Architecture: Traditional AI models have typically been narrow and specialized –

for example, a vision model classifies images, or a chatbot is trained solely for dialogue. Such

systems don't “understand” the world beyond their narrow inputs. In contrast, world models are

designed to be general-purpose simulators. Technically, they combine multiple components

(vision, memory, language, control) into one coherent system that can learn world dynamics. A

classic traditional approach might use separate modules and human-crafted rules for perception

and planning, whereas a world model learns an internal representation of the environment end-

to-end arionresearch.com arionresearch.com. For instance, a world model for robotics will

encode sensory inputs into a latent space, use a dynamics model (often a recurrent neural net or

transformer) to predict future states, and a controller to decide actions – effectively enabling the

AI to “imagine” outcomes internally arionresearch.com arionresearch.com. This is very

different from a traditional robot controller that reacts to sensor data with pre-programmed

responses.

Multimodality and Data: World models embrace multimodal learning. They ingest images,

text, audio, video, and even haptic signals, whereas older AI systems often handled one modality

at a time. By training on broad data (e.g. reading text while watching videos), world models can

correlate across modalities – gaining a richer understanding (e.g. associating the word “cat” with

images, sounds, movements of cats). The result is an AI that has a more holistic context, closer

to how humans accumulate knowledge. Traditional models lacked this breadth; even a strong

7

language model of the 2010s had no vision, and a vision model had no notion of text or physics.

Large world models today use architectures like transformers extended to sequences of images

or 3D data, enabling long-horizon temporal reasoning (tracking how a scene changes over

time) which older feed-forward networks could not do research.aimultiple.com

research.aimultiple.com. Technically, new mechanisms (e.g. ring attention or advanced

memory modules) are being developed to handle very long input sequences and maintain state

over time research.aimultiple.com – a crucial need for world models that simulate entire video

sequences or dialogues lasting hours.

Learning and Generalization: Traditional AI often required task-specific training with labeled

data. For example, to build a speech recognizer, one collected speech transcripts and trained a

model; to play a game, one coded rules or did narrow reinforcement learning in that game. World

models, by contrast, rely heavily on self-supervised learning at scale. They learn patterns from

unlabeled data (predicting the next word, or the next video frame) – which endows them with

general world knowledge that can be adapted to many tasks. This means technically a

foundation/world model can be fine-tuned for a new task with relatively few examples,

leveraging its broad pretraining. This is a major shift: rather than training a new model from

scratch for each problem, a single large model serves as a base for many tasks en.wikipedia.org

en.wikipedia.org. In practice, this has led to emergent capabilities – e.g. a single GPT-4 model

can translate languages, answer science questions, write code, etc., whereas achieving all those

tasks with separate traditional models would be far more laborious. World models tend to

generalize better to scenarios they weren't explicitly trained on, because they capture underlying

structures of the world (like physics or grammar) rather than just memorizing narrow patterns

arionresearch.com. For example, a world-model-based autonomous car can handle an unusual

situation (e.g. a new type of obstacle) by reasoning from first principles of physical dynamics,

whereas a traditional system might fail if that scenario wasn't in its programmed data.

However, technical complexity of world models is higher. They have orders of magnitude more

parameters (often billions to trillions) and require sophisticated engineering (for distributed

training, handling diverse data, etc.). Ensuring consistency across modalities and maintaining an

accurate “mental simulation” is an open challenge – world models can sometimes hallucinate or

diverge if their internal model doesn't match reality (the “reality gap” issue arionresearch.com).

Researchers are actively working on improving fidelity of simulations and integrating explicit

knowledge (e.g. hybrid neuro-symbolic methods) to address these issues.

Economic Considerations

Development Cost: World models are immensely expensive to develop compared to traditional

AI systems. Training a foundation model with tens of billions of parameters on terabytes of data

can cost millions of dollars in cloud compute and require specialized hardware. In fact, the most

advanced models today are estimated to cost on the order of hundreds of millions of dollars in

overall expenses (data collection, engineering, GPU compute) en.wikipedia.org. By contrast, a

traditional machine learning model for a specific task can often be trained on a single server or

small cluster in days, at far lower cost. This economic barrier means that only well-funded

organizations (Big Tech companies, government-sponsored labs, or heavily funded startups)

can afford to build true state-of- the-art world models from scratch. This is reflected in the

8

concentration of foundation model development in a few countries and companies as noted

earlier (U.S. and China primarily) atlanticcouncil.org. The supply chain for world models –

including access to cutting-edge chips (GPUs, TPUs), large-scale datasets, and expert talent –

has itself become a strategic asset (leading, for example, the U.S. to impose export controls on

high-end AI chips to rivals).

Deployment and Scale Economies: While expensive to create, world models yield economies

of scale in deployment. A single foundation model can be deployed across many applications

and tasks, amortizing its training cost. For instance, once OpenAI trained GPT-4 (at great

expense), that same model now powers hundreds of products and services via API – from

customer service bots to coding assistants – each of which would have required separate models

before. Fine-tuning a foundation model for a specific task or domain is relatively cost-

efficient (requiring a small fraction of the original training effort) en.wikipedia.org. This means

enterprises can adapt a pre-trained world model to their needs instead of paying the full cost of

development. In an economic sense, foundation models act like a new type of

infrastructure: high upfront cost, low marginal cost for reuse. This is shifting the AI market.

Companies with the largest models can offer AI “as a service” (e.g. via cloud APIs), potentially

undercutting smaller competitors who cannot match the performance. On the other hand, open-

source models (like Meta's LLaMA, Stability AI's image models) are providing more accessible

alternatives that level the playing field to some extent. We are seeing a trend of industries

pooling resources to train large models (e.g. the EU considering an “AI factory” to host models

for European companies atlanticcouncil.org atlanticcouncil.org), recognizing that not every

entity can afford its own GPT-4 equivalent.

Operational and Labor Impact: Deploying world models can bring efficiency gains and cost

savings in various sectors, but also requires investment in new infrastructure (for inference

serving, fine-tuning, etc.). For example, using a world model for customer support (like an AI

agent that can handle many inquiries) can reduce labor costs, but the organization must invest in

integrating that model and curating its outputs. There's also an energy cost: large models

consume significant electricity both in training and serving, raising questions about cost and

sustainability. From a labor economics perspective, foundation models can automate or

accelerate high-skilled work (coding, writing, design), which is both an opportunity for

productivity and a disruption to traditional job roles. The World Economic Forum estimates

that 23% of jobs could change in the next five years due to AI adoption, reflecting how pervasive

these models are expected to become in workflows thoughtsfromthedatafront.com. In short,

world models are driving an economic shift where AI becomes a utility – potentially a large

growth sector (the generative AI market is forecast to grow from $37B in 2024 to over $200B

by 2030) and an ubiquitous part of value creation in businesse s.

Strategic and Policy Implications

World models have quickly become viewed as strategic assets on national and corporate levels,

much like critical infrastructure or advanced weaponry. The reasoning is that the entity with

the most advanced AI capabilities could gain significant advantages economically, militarily, and

in influence. Here are key strategic dimensions:

9

•

National Competitiveness: Nations are explicitly integrating AI foundation models into

their strategic plans. The United States and China, in particular, see leadership in AI as

a determinant of future global power. In July 2025, the U.S. government released

“America's AI Action Plan,” highlighting AI as a decisive frontier of competition and

calling to marshal resources (from deregulating industries to boosting domestic chip

manufacturing) to maintain leadership atlanticcouncil.org chinausfocus.com. A few days

later, China announced its own national AI action plan at the World AI Conference,

emphasizing rapid commercialization and international cooperation to position itself as a

global AI hub chinausfocus.com chinausfocus.com. Both plans center on foundation

models as key technology – whether it's the U.S. aiming to export its AI models to allies

while denying them to adversaries chinausfocus.com, or China aiming to build and

spread its own models (with fewer political conditions attached) to courting the Global

South chinausfocus.com. The transatlantic divide also illustrates strategy: the U.S. relies

on private sector-led model development with light regulation, whereas the EU (which

lags in model development) focuses on regulation and consortium efforts to catch up

atlanticcouncil.org atlanticcouncil.org. Fundamentally, advanced world models are seen

as dual-use technology (civil and military). They can drive economic growth (AI

innovations, new industries) but also enhance intelligence analysis, cyber operations,

autonomous defense systems – so governments are keen to ensure they are not

outpaced. For example, by 2023 the U.S. Department of Defense was exploring using

open foundation models internally for advantages like operating AI on secure

infrastructure and customizing it to defense needs csis.org.

•

Corporate Strategy and IP: For tech corporations, owning a cutting-edge world model

(or the platform it runs on) can be a ticket to market dominance. We see intense

competition between a few firms – sometimes called an “AI arms race.” Only a handful

of companies (OpenAI/Microsoft, Google, Meta, Anthropic, a few in China) can

currently build the very top models. This confers a quasi-monopoly or oligopoly power in

the short term. These models are often treated as highly valuable intellectual property: for

instance, OpenAI has not open-sourced GPT-4, and details of its training are closely

guarded. Companies must strategize how open or closed to be. Meta's open-source

LLaMA was a bold move to gain ecosystem influence (getting many researchers on

board) rather than monetizing directly. Google and OpenAI have more closed

approaches, offering pay- as-you-go APIs. The economics of AI thus shape strategy:

companies can lose relevance if they bet on the wrong approach (e.g., focusing only on

small models when larger ones prove superior, or vice versa). We've also seen strategic

partnerships and investments (Microsoft–OpenAI, Amazon–Anthropic, Google investing

in UIPath and others) aimed at securing access to the best models.

•

Resource Control: The race for world models has brought issues like compute

sovereignty and data control to the forefront. Countries and companies worry about

reliance on others' models – e.g., Europe largely uses U.S.-built models, raising

sovereignty concerns atlanticcouncil.org. This is driving initiatives to build domestic

compute infrastructure (the EU raising €20B for “AI compute factories”

atlanticcouncil.org) and discussions of international standards and governance.

Compute resources (top-end GPUs, advanced chips) are somewhat analogous to nuclear

enrichment in the AI context – they determine who can build frontier models. That's why

export controls on AI chips have been implemented by the U.S. and allies targeting

10

China. Strategically, there is also the question of AI safety and ethics: world models are

powerful but can be misused (for disinformation, cyberattacks, etc.), so governments are

moving to create guardrails. The EU's upcoming AI Act will impose requirements on

“foundation model” providers (such as requiring disclosure of training data and

mitigating harmful outputs). The U.S. issued an Executive Order (Oct 2025) on safe

deployment of AI, including defining foundation models and stipulating testing for

secu rity risks en.wikipedia.org. These policies reflect that world models are now seen

through a strategic lens – not just tech products, but something to be managed for

national security, similar to how critical infrastructure or strategic industries are handled.

In summary, world models outshine traditional AI technically through breadth and predictive

power, but demand far more resources and come with far-reaching economic and strategic

implications. Traditional AI might be likened to individual tools, whereas large world models

are becoming an AI infrastructure – with all the transformative potential and challenges that

infrastructure brings.

Capabilities and Applications Where World Models Excel

Thanks to their generality and scale, world models are demonstrating superior performance or

potential in many areas compared to earlier approaches. Below are specific

capabilities/applications where they currently outperform or are expected to:

•

Natural Language Understanding and Generation: Large language-based world

models (like GPT-4, Claude, etc.) have achieved unprecedented performance in

language tasks. They can understand nuanced queries, generate human-like coherent

responses, translate between dozens of languages, and even solve reasoning puzzles in

text – tasks that used to require separate specialized models (or were outright impossible

for AI) are now handled by one foundation model. For example, GPT-4 can write code,

compose essays, and answer medical and legal questions at a high level, surpassing

earlier NLP systems on standard benchmarks. These models exhibit few-shot learning –

given a couple examples of a new task in a prompt, they often outperform traditional

models that were explicitly trained for that task. This versatility and fluency in language

generation is enabling applications like advanced chatbots, AI writing assistants, and

complex knowledge query systems that search and reason over large document

collections. Traditional rule-based chatbots or FAQ systems cannot compete with the

adaptability and depth of response that an LLM-based system provides. We've also seen

Google DeepMind: Following Google's internal restructuring, DeepMind is now

combined with Google's resources, and their roadmap is to produce next-gen foundation

models that leverage both DeepMind's research prowess and Google's scale. The

anticipated Gemini model is one clear goal: it's said to be a multi-modal, highly capable

model incorporating techniques from AlphaGo (like planning and reinforcement

learning) into an LLM, potentially allowing it to do things like tool use, math problem

solving, and strategic planning beyond what current LLMs can do. DeepMind has also

been strong in embodied AI, so we expect their roadmap includes agents like Robotic

Transformer (RT-2) which they already developed – an AI that can control robots using

21

knowledge from web data en.wikipedia.org. Future iterations may yield robots that learn

new tasks via language commands and world model reasoning (e.g., you tell a robot chef

in 2030 “make this new dish”, and it uses a world-model to figure out the steps and

execute safely). DeepMind's philosophy often emphasizes fundamental

breakthroughs (like solving protein folding, mastering Go, etc.), so their internal goals

likely include major milestones: perhaps solving something like efficient AI-driven

weather forecasting (they already did some work on nowcasting), or creating AI that can

design other machines (AI-assisted engineering). On the commercial side, since they are

within Google, these advances will feed into Google's products: expect more powerful

Google Search with AI (a “Search Generative Experience” that gives synthesized

answers), Google Cloud offering custom model training (they already offer the Vertex AI

platform with model garden), and AI features across Android/Assistant (to keep Google's

ecosystem competitive with the likes of ChatGPT and Siri). DeepMind is also one of the

leaders in AI safety research (they have a dedicated team for technical AI safety), so

part of their roadmap is to develop techniques to verify and control the behavior of

increasingly powerful models – e.g., scalable oversight, interpretability tools to peer into

the “thought process” of world models, etc. By 2030, Google DeepMind likely aims to

deliver an AI that can be a general problem-solver integrated into Google's services – an

AI that might plan your day via Google Assistant, drive your car via Waymo, and

accelerate scientific research via cloud tools.

•

Meta (Facebook) AI: Meta's roadmap appears to diverge from the closed proprietary

model of OpenAI/Google and lean into an open innovation approach. In 2023, Meta

released LLaMA models openly to researchers, and in 2024 it partnered with Microsoft to

release LLaMA 2 with a commercial-friendly license. We anticipate Meta continuing this

strategy: developing large models (including a rumored LLaMA 3 or multimodal model)

and open-sourcing them. The rationale is that Meta doesn't sell AI APIs as a primary

business; instead, it benefits if AI is widespread and can be embedded in its social

platforms and devices. Meta is heavily investing in augmented reality and the

metaverse – and AI is key to those experiences. By 2030, Meta envisions AR glasses

that are like an always-on world-model-powered assistant, whispering information about

the world you see (identifying people, translating signboards, suggesting restaurants as

you walk down a street). This requires on-device or efficiently streamed world models

that understand vision, language, and context. Meta's research includes projects

like ImageBind (which learned to align data from image, audio, text, depth, etc.),

pointing toward a multimodal world model for AR klover.ai. They also built SAM

(Segment Anything Model) which can identify objects in images without specific

training, useful for AR and robotics. Corporate roadmap statements from Mark

Zuckerberg have emphasized building an “AI person” that could eventually act as a

versatile assistant in the metaverse – ess entially a world-model-driven avatar that

interacts naturally in virtual environments. With LeCun's departure to focus on world

models, Meta may double down on hiring or partnering to ensure they don't fall behind in

embodied AI; or they might acquire promising startups in that space. In social media,

Meta is integrating generative AI to help creators (e.g., AI tools for image editing in

Instagram, AI chat agents on Messenger). We'll likely see personalized world models for

content recommendation – imagine AI that better understands a user's preferences by

modeling their interactions over time, potentially reducing the echo-chamber effects by

22

truly grasping a user's intent (one can hope). By 2030, Meta's success may hinge on

blending AI and AR – if they manage to create compelling AR glasses with an AI

assistant that people love, they could lead a new market. In summary, Meta's roadmap

uses open model release to spur innovation and adoption, while focusing internally on AI

for immersive, interactive experiences aligned with their metaverse vision.

•

Microsoft: As a close partner to OpenAI and a provider of enterprise technology,

Microsoft's roadmap for world models is entwined with both leveraging OpenAI's

innovations and building its own value-add. Microsoft is integrating GPT-4 (and

successors) deeply into its product suite: Office 365's Copilot (AI that can draft emails,

create PowerPoints, analyze Excel data via natural language) is a flagship offering. By

2030, Microsoft aims for every professional to have an AI copilot for their tasks –

effectively a world-model-driven agent that knows your work context (files, calendar,

team chats) and can automate or assist with a huge range of knowledge work.

Microsoft's Azure cloud is positioning itself as the platform for foundation models:

they offer pretrained models (through Azure OpenAI Service) and tools for fine-tuning

and deploying them securely. Expect Azure to host an increasing variety of models (some

possibly Microsoft's own – they have the “Turing” family for language and “Florence”

for vision, etc.) covering different modalities and sizes to cater to different customer

needs. Microsoft also sees AI as critical for developers: their GitHub Copilot is already

widely adopted among programmers to autocomplete and generate code; future versions

will likely handle larger chunks of software design, maybe even generating whole

modules based on natural language specifications. On the research front, Microsoft

Research works on AI alignment, including interpretability (they published work on how

transformers handle logic) and robustness (adversarial testing of models), which will feed

into safer deployment in their products. Also notable is Microsoft's involvement in AI

governance: they've publicly supported regulations and announced their own

commitments (like requiring AI systems to have disclosures, not using AI for unlawful

surveillance, etc.). So they see responsible AI as part of the roadmap, likely to

differentiate their enterprise offerings as “trustworthy.” One wildcard is Microsoft's

hardware strategy: with OpenAI, they co-designed an AI supercomputer and have

invested in custom AI chips (codenamed Athena) to reduce reliance on NVIDIA. By

2030, Microsoft could have a significant hardware stack optimized for world models,

giving it cost and performance advantages in running these services. Overall, Microsoft

will continue to be the major commercializer of world models, aiming to embed them in

the daily workflow of millions of users and offering the backbone infrastructure for the

AI-powered economy.

•

Amazon: Amazon has been a quieter player in foundation models until recently, but it is

now moving assertively. In late 2023, Amazon invested $4B in Anthropic, securing

access to Claude models for AWS. Amazon's roadmap involves making AWS the go-to

cloud for AI just as it is for standard computing. Their Bedrock service offers multiple

foundation models (from Anthropic, AI21 Labs, Stability AI, and Amazon's own if

available) via API, targeting enterprise developers who want to incorporate AI without

building models from scratch. We can expect Amazon to invest in its own models as well

– they reportedly have a project training a GPT-4-scale model (perhaps focused on e-

commerce knowledge or conversational abilities for Alexa). Speaking of Alexa, by 2030

Amazon surely wants a rejuvenated AI assistant that is far smarter. Alexa pioneered

23

voice assistants but fell behind in IQ; a foundation model-powered Alexa could not only

control smart homes but also engage in meaningful dialogue, make inferences (e.g.,

proactively suggest reordering items you might be low on, based on its model of your

household), and serve as a shopping advisor. Commerce is a key angle: Amazon will

deploy world models to improve search and recommendations on its retail platform –

e.g., understanding product reviews and customer queries with LLMs to return better

results, or even generating summaries (“AI, has this product had any safety issues?” ->

model reads all reviews and answers). They are also likely to use world models in

logistics (optimizing routes, predicting demand shifts) and supply chain simulations –

areas Amazon has huge data for and where even small efficiency gains save lots of

money. Given Amazon's device ecosystem (Echo, Ring, etc.), they might integrate

lightweight models on edge devices for quick responses and privacy (with heavy lifting

done in cloud). Another aspect is Amazon's internal use: they could utilize world models

for coding (they launched CodeWhisperer to compete with GitHub Copilot), for customer

service (AI agents in call centers), and more. Security and reliability will be emphasized,

si nce AWS's corporate clients will demand it. By 2030, Amazon likely envisions itself as

the one-stop shop for AI in the cloud, with a stable of competitive models (from partners

and in-house) covering different needs (maybe an Amazon vision model for image

analysis on AWS, etc.), much like how Amazon stocks many brands. Their ultimate goal

would be that whenever a company decides “we need AI in our product,” they

instinctively go to AWS and choose from Amazon's world model catalog and tools.

•

Notable Startups and Others: Beyond the giants, many startups have their own

roadmaps aiming to carve out niches or challenge the incumbents:

o Anthropic (USA) – Having secured major funding, Anthropic aims to build ever

safer and more powerful language models. Their roadmap includes Claude-Next,

which they claim could be 5–10× more capable than current models if they invest

on the order of $1B or more in training. Anthropic focuses on a technique called

“constitutional AI” to align models with human values without constant human

feedback, and this will likely be refined. By 2030, Anthropic might position itself

as the safety leader – the company you go to if you want an AI that is less likely

to go rogue or say harmful things, even if it's slightly less edgy than a

competitor's model. They may also expand beyond pure text – possibly into

multimodal (they've hinted at interest) – to keep up with others. Anthropic's

partnership with Amazon means their models will be widely accessible, giving

them a distribution channel to many enterprises.

o Inflection AI (USA) – This startup founded by former DeepMind leader Mustafa

Suleyman is focused on personal AI agents. They released Pi, an AI “companion”

chatbot. Inflection is reportedly working on large models (they bought tons of

NVIDIA GPUs) specialized for dialog and personal assistance. By 2030,

Inflection's vision might be to provide each person with a bespoke AI that knows

them intimately (within privacy boundaries), akin to the AI in the movie Her but

grounded. Their strategy is to differentiate from generic assistants by

emphasizing emotional intelligence and long-term learning about the user. This

could push the envelope in personalization: for example, your Inflection AI in

2030 might organize your life, coach you through challenges, and interface with

other services, acting as a true digital proxy.

24

o Adept AI (USA) – Adept is building AI agents that can use software tools like

humans do (clicking, typing, copying info between apps). Their roadmap likely

sees world models as the brain behind an AI office assistant that can automate

any computer task by observing and imitating how humans use software. By

2030, Adept could offer AI workers that handle tedious digital workflows (e.g.,

processing invoices, updating CRM entries) by simply telling them in natural

language. This approach extends foundation models with an action interface (like

an API to control mouse/keyboard in a virtual environment), which if successful,

can greatly increase workplace efficiency.

o Sector-Specific Startups: There are many focusing on applying world models in

specific fields – e.g., Jua for weather (as discussed, aiming to revolutionize

forecasting), Healx or Insilico Medicine using AI for drug discovery (with

foundation models predicting molecular properties and suggesting compound

designs), Abacus.AI and others for finance (using large models to detect fraud or

forecast markets). Their roadmaps often involve customizing general models to

specialized data and needs, effectively building domain-specific world

models (e.g., a model that “understands” chemistry deeply, beyond a general

model's knowledge). By 2030, we'll see mature AI solutions in these sectors that

outperform human experts in narrow tasks (like diagnosing certain diseases from

medical images with world-model-level training on global data, or optimizing a

supply chain with AI agents negotiating with each other in simulation).

o Open-Source Community (LAION, Eleuther, etc.): The open-source AI

community will continue to produce competitive models, which is part of many

organizations' roadmaps to avoid vendor lock-in. We might see an openly

developed “GPT-4 equivalent” by 2026–2027, if trends continue (as of 2023,

models like LLaMA-65B and Stable Diffusion are open). Open-source models

empower smaller companies and researchers globally, and likely there will

be community-driven world models for various languages and modalities by

This could be analogous to the Linux revolution for operating systems – an

ecosystem of foundation models that are free to use and adapt, which many

corporate roadmaps will include as a hedge or cost-saving (since they avoid

paying API fees to others).

In summary, corporate roadmaps across the board place world models at the heart of future

offerings: from consumer products (smart assistants, AR glasses) to enterprise software (AI

copilots, automation) to cloud platforms (AI-as-a-service). The competition is both in model

capabilities and in how to deliver them (safely, cheaply, ubiquitously). By 2030, we expect to

see a handful of extremely powerful world models (on the order of GPT-5++ or Gemini 2.0)

that a wide range of services are built on, as well as many specialized models. The leaders like

OpenAI, Google, Meta are essentially racing to create an AI that can understand and

do almost anything (within alignment constraints). Their roadmaps reflect an ultimate goal of

integrating such an AI deeply into our lives – augmenting human intelligence and

productivity. Startups and smaller players, meanwhile, target niches or novel interaction

paradigms to differentiate (since it's hard to beat the giants in a straight-up model size race).

25

One thing is certain: collaboration and integration are recurring themes. Companies are

partnering (e.g., joint model training ventures, API integrations), and national strategies often

mention public-private cooperation (the U.S. plan, for instance, involves private sector leading

innovation with government facilitating chinausfocus.com chinausfocus.com). The coming five

years will likely see deeper integration of world models not just into software, but into the fabric

of society – education systems teaching students how to use AI wisely, enterprises restructuring

job roles around AI, and governments using AI for policymaking assistance.

Finally, while the opportunities are immense, all actors are also grappling with

the responsibility that comes with world models. Issues of bias, privacy, security, and

employment are being addressed in roadmaps via technical fixes and policies. For example,

many companies now have AI ethics teams and publish transparency reports. As world models

become more entrenched, these governance aspects will move from side notes to central parts of

roadmaps (e.g., commitments to verifiable compliance with regulations, robust AI evaluation

before deployment, etc.). It's an exciting and challenging era ahead – one in which world

models will increasingly shape the trajectory of technology and society.
