Abstract and Figures

Industry 4.0 and 5.0 have led to the extensive implementation of Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML). AI and ML signify a significant breakthrough in numerous fields by enabling more efficient data processing, offering enhancements across various services, and automation to replicate the learning process of machines, thereby enhancing system accuracy. In machining processes, AI and ML play crucial roles in predicting cutting forces, tool wear, and optimizing machining parameters. By employing advanced ML systems, machining operations can achieve longer cutting tool lifespan and increased efficiency. Additionally, these systems enable the prediction and enhancement of surface quality in machined components, contributing to overall part quality improvement. Furthermore, ML techniques are instrumental in analyzing and reducing power consumption during machining operations by predicting the energy consumption patterns of machine tools. This paper reviews the applications of AI and ML in machining operations and suggests future research directions. By examining recent achievements in the available literature, it aims to advance the research field by offering innovative concepts and approaches for integrating AI and ML into machining industries.

AI and ML in Machining

The use of AI and ML in machining operations represents

a transformative shift in manufacturing, oering the poten-

tial for enhanced eciency, productivity, and quality. At its

core, this integration relies on a conceptual framework in

which computational algorithms allow machines to make

decisions on the basis of input data [ 9]. The learning process

in machines involves collection, preprocessing, training and

evaluation, and deployment, with data serving as the founda-

tion for training accurate ML models. Data collection serves

as the initial step in the learning process, where relevant

information about the machining process is gathered from

various sources, including sensors, Internet of Things (IoT)

devices, and historical databases [ 10]. These data encom-

pass parameters such as cutting forces, tool wear, material

properties, and environmental conditions, providing valu-

able insights into the underlying dynamics of machining

operations [ 11]. However, raw data collected from disparate

sources may be noisy, incomplete, or redundant, necessitat-

ing preprocessing steps to clean, normalize, and transform it

into a format suitable for analysis [ 12].

Preprocessing techniques such as data imputation, out-

lier detection, and feature scaling are applied to enhance the

quality and usability of the data, ensuring that it accurately

reects the underlying features of the machining process.

Once the data have been preprocessed, they are used to train

ML models via supervised, unsupervised, or reinforcement

learning techniques, depending on the nature of the task and

available data [ 11– 13]. Figure 2 shows ML methods.

In machining operations, unsupervised learning tech-

niques can be employed to identify clusters of similar

machining conditions or tool wear patterns, facilitating

anomaly detection and process optimization [ 14– 16]. Rein-

forcement learning represents a third paradigm of ML, where

the model learns to make sequential decisions through inter-

action with an environment. In the context of machining,

reinforcement learning algorithms can be used to optimize

machining parameters on the basis of feedback received

from the machining process [ 17]. Supervised learning may

be employed in machining, especially as manufacturing

processes become more complex and the demand for preci-

sion and eciency increases. Supervised learning helps in

determining the optimal combination of cutting parameters

to achieve specic goals like minimizing machining time,

reducing tool wear, or achieving the best surface nish.

By iteratively exploring dierent action sequences and

observing their impact on machining performance, the

model can learn to adapt its behavior to achieve desired

objectives such as minimizing tool wear or maximizing the

material removal rate.

Once the ML model has been trained, it is evaluated

using separate validation data to assess its performance and

general capabilities [ 18]. The eectiveness of ML models

is frequently measured using metrics such as accuracy,

precision, recall, and the F1-score, providing insights into

their predictive power and robustness. Model evaluation is

a critical step in ML which identies potential issues such

as overtting, undertting, or data leakage that may com-

promise the model's performance in real-world scenarios.

Upon successful validation, the trained ML model can be

deployed in real-world machining environments to make

predictions or decisions in real-time. Deployment involves

integrating the ML model into existing manufacturing sys-

tems, ensuring seamless interoperability with hardware and

software components [ 19].

In the context of machining operations, ML models can be

deployed on edge devices such as computer numerical con-

trol (CNC), programmable logic controller (PLC), or indus-

trial robots allowing for immediate decision-making and

inference in real-time. Continuous monitoring and feedback

mechanisms may be established to further rene the model

performance over time, ensuring ongoing optimization and

adaptation to changing machining conditions. The role of

data and algorithms in improving system accuracy cannot

be overstated in the context of AI and ML-based machin-

ing operations [ 20]. T o train machine learning models,

high-quality data sets are needed. However, collecting and

curating large amounts of data can be dicult in practice,

especially in highly dynamic and heterogeneous machining

environments. Moreover, the selection and optimization of

algorithms play key roles in evaluating the performance of

ML models, with dierent algorithms exhibiting varying

levels of accuracy, eciency, and scalability [ 21]. Figure 3

shows the data-driven machining process ow with an AI/

ML integration owchart.

Several ML algorithms have been applied to address spe-

cic challenges such as predicting cutting forces, monitoring

tool wear, optimizing machining parameters, and improving

surface quality in machined components [ 22]. For example,

articial neural network (ANN) is well-suited for modeling

complex nonlinear relationships, whereas decision trees are

interpretable and easy to understand, making them suitable

for tasks requiring transparency and explainability. Further-

more, recent advancements in hardware acceleration, par-

allel computing, and distributed processing have facilitated

the deployment of complex ML models on resource-con-

strained platforms, enabling real-time inference and deci-

sion-making in practical machining scenarios [ 23].

Process Parameters and Variables

The process parameters constitute the foundational ele-

ments that dene and characterize machining operations.

These parameters encompass a wide array of physical

quantities and operational settings that inuence the behav-

ior, performance, and outcomes of the machining process.

The key process parameters include the feed rate, material

hardness, depth of cut, tool material, tool geometry, cut-

ting speed and coolant ow rate [ 24]. Each parameter has a

substantial eect on various aspects of machining, such as

tool wear, material removal rates, surface nish, and forces.

Process optimization endeavors to systematically adjust and

ne-tune these parameters to enhance overall process per-

formance, productivity, and cost-eectiveness [ 25]. In con-

trast to process parameters, process variables encompass

the measurable signals and characteristics observed during

machining operations. These variables provide real-time

understanding of the dynamic behavior, performance, and

condition of the machining process [ 26]. Common process

variables include cutting forces, temperatures, vibrations,

surface roughness, tool wear, and dimensional accuracy.

By monitoring these variables, manufacturers can assess

the health, stability, and eciency of the machining sys-

tem, identify anomalies or deviations, and make decisions

to optimize performance [ 27].

Applications of AI and ML in Machining

Operations

AI and ML are increasingly becoming part of the machin-

ing process to improve precision, eciency, accuracy, and

productivity. Predictive maintenance models analyze sensor

data of equipment to schedule maintenance preemptively,

whereas ML algorithms optimise process parameters based

on previous data, minimizing tool wear, and improving sur-

face smoothness [ 37]. AI systems detect anomalies in real-

time ensuring product quality, and ML techniques automate

quality control through image analysis, thus reducing scrap.

Tool wear predictive algorithms mostly include physical

models, signal processing and ML that maximize tool life

on the basis of identied optimal machining parameters.

These algorithms optimize tool life by enabling data-driven

decisions regarding tool maintenance and replacement. This

avoids catastrophic failure of cutting tools and thus reduces

downtime [ 38]. Adaptive control systems adjust parameters

dynamically based on feedback, leading to improved accu-

racy whereas AI-driven supply chain optimization enhances

inventory management and reduces disruptions. These uses

of AI/ML methods in machining operations contribute to

increased exibility and competitiveness in modern manu-

facturing [ 39]. The use of AI and ML approaches in machin-

ing operations is displayed in T ables 2 and 3.

Sensors and Data Acquisition Systems

Eective data acquisition lies at the core of leveraging data-

driven approaches in metal cutting. A range of sensors, IoT

devices, and data-gathering systems are used in modern

industrial environments to gather simultaneous data from

various sources. These sensors and devices are strategi-

cally deployed to capture key process parameters and vari-

ables, providing a comprehensive view of the machining

process [ 28]. Common types of sensors used in machining

include force sensors, temperature sensors, accelerometers,

displacement sensors, and proximity sensors. Data acqui-

sition systems interface with these sensors to collect, pro-

cess, and transmit data to centralized storage platforms for

further analysis and processing [ 29]. Centralized data stor-

age platforms play a key role in managing and storing the

vast volumes of data generated during machining opera-

tions. These platforms provide scalable, reliable, and secure

storage solutions for storing diverse data types, including

sensor readings, machine status updates, production logs,

and quality inspection results. Cloud-based databases, on-

premises servers, and distributed storage architectures are

among the common storage mechanisms employed in mod-

ern manufacturing environments [ 30]. Centralized data stor-

age enables seamless access, retrieval, and sharing of data

across dierent departments and stakeholders, facilitating

collaboration, decision-making, and performance monitor-

ing [ 31].

Conclusions

AI and ML are used in a variety of industrial applications

to enhance the performance of industrial processes. It helps

in monitoring and predicting tool wear, surface quality and

overall maintenance of the machine etc. The present inves-

tigation has led to the following conclusions:

●

Industrial processes benet from the integration of ML

and AI, enhancing performance across various sectors.

Research has explored diverse ML and AI applica-

tions to improve accuracy and eciency in machining

operations.

●

ML and AI systems play pivotal roles in machining ad-

vancements, encompassing tasks such as minimizing

machine downtime, optimizing parameters, predicting

AI and ML algorithms directly into CNC machines or con-

trollers, these systems can continuously optimize cutting

processes, adapt to changing conditions, and ensure con-

sistent part quality. As AI and ML technologies become

more ubiquitous in machining, there is a growing oppor-

tunity for human-machine collaboration. Future research

could explore how AI-driven systems can augment human

expertise, assist operators in decision-making, and facili-

tate intuitive interactions between humans and machines.

This collaborative approach not only enhances productiv-

ity but also fosters a more ergonomic and inclusive work

environment.

On the other hand, the primary challenge in execut-

ing AI and ML in machining operations is the availability

of high-quality data. Machining processes generate vast

amounts of data, but ensuring their accuracy, completeness,

and relevance for training ML models can be challenging.

Moreover, data collection eorts may be hindered by limita-

tions in sensor technology or inaccessible data from legacy

machines. Raw machining data often require extensive pre-

processing before they can be used for training ML models.

This preprocessing involves tasks such as cleaning, nor-

malization, feature extraction, and fusion of heterogeneous

data sources. Integrating data from disparate sources and

formats adds complexity to the preprocessing pipeline and

requires specialized expertise. ML models trained on spe-

cic machining datasets may struggle to generalize to new

or unseen operating conditions, materials, or machining

processes. Achieving model transferability across dierent

machining setups or environments is a signicant challenge,

as variations in equipment, tooling, and operating param-

eters can aect model performance.

transparency and explainability may raise concerns regard-

ing model reliability, trustworthiness, and regulatory com-

pliance. Building and deploying ML models for machining

Driven by the ongoing migration towards Industry 4.0, the increasing adoption of artificial intelligence (AI) has empowered smart manufacturing and digital transformation. AI enhances the migration towards industry 4.0 through AI-based decision-making by analyzing real-time data to optimize different processes such as production planning, predictive maintenance, quality control etc., thus guaranteeing reduced costs, high precision, efficiency and accuracy. This paper explores AI-driven smart manufacturing, revolutionizing traditional approaches and unlocking new possibilities throughout the major phases of the industrial equipment lifecycle. Through a comprehensive review, we delve into a wide range of AI techniques employed to tackle challenges such as optimizing process control, machining parameters, facilitating decision-making, and elevating maintenance strategies within the major phases of an industrial equipment lifecycle. These phases encompass design, manufacturing, maintenance, and recycling/retrofitting. As reported in the 2022 McKinsey Global Survey (https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai-in-2022-and-a-half-decade-in-review), the adoption of AI has witnessed more than a two-fold increase since 2017. High-quality machining is a crucial aspect of contemporary manufacturing technology due to the vast demand for precision machining for parts made from hardened tool steels and super alloys globally in the aerospace, automobile, and medical sectors. The necessity to upheave production efficiency and quality enhancement at minimum cost requires deep knowledge of this cutting process and development of machine learning-based modeling technique, adept in providing essential tools for design, planning, and incorporation in the machining processes. This research aims to develop a predictive surface roughness model and optimize its process parameters for ultra-precision hard-turning finishing operation. Ultra-precision hard-turning experiments were carried out on AISI D2 of HRC 62. The response surface method (RSM) was applied to understand the effect of process parameters on surface roughness and carry out optimization. Based on the data gained from experiments, machine learning models and algorithms were developed with support vector machine (SVM), Gaussian process relation (GPR), adaptive neuro-fuzzy inference system (ANFIS), and artificial neural network (ANN) for the prediction of surface roughness. The results show that all machine learning models gave excellent predictive accuracy with an average MAPE value of 7.38%. The validation tests were also statistically significant, with ANFIS and ANN having MAPE values of 9.98% and 3.43%, respectively. Additional validation tests for the models with new experimental data indicate average R, RMSE, and MAPE values of 0.78, 0.19, and 36.17%, respectively, which are satisfactory. The RSM analysis shows that the feed is the most significant factor for minimizing surface roughness Rɑ, among the process parameters, with 92% influence, and optimal cutting conditions were found to be cutting speed = 100 m/min, feed = 0.025 mm/rev, and depth of cut = 0.09 mm, respectively. This finding can be helpful in the decision-making on process parameters in the precision machining industry.

Porosity is a key variable for hydrocarbon reservoirs evaluation. It can be directly determined in laboratory tests using core samples or calculated indirectly from well logs. However, these methods are expensive and time consuming affecting the cost of supply of oil produced. Monitoring drilling variables in real time as a borehole is drilled can provide a cheap, initial insight to various rock formation properties. Investigating the relationships between rock porosity and drilling variables is therefore important. This study applies machines learning (ML) relate rock porosity to a range of measured drilling variables in several types of reservoir. Four ML models are evaluated: K-nearest neighbor (KNN), random forest (RF), support vector regression (SVR), and eXtreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost). A measured drilling and porosity dataset from the Rhourd Chegga oilfield (SE Algeria) is compiled from its multi-zone reservoir system. The drilling variables from nine vertical wellbores considered are: weight on bit, torque, standpipe pressure, drill string rotation speed, rate of penetration and its inverse, pump rate and bit depth. The recorded data is derived from three distinct reservoir zones: Trias (T1), Rhourd Chegga sandstone (RDC), and the Quartzite Hamra tight reservoir (QH). Porosity predictions were derived in two ways: (1) for each reservoir separately; (2) for the three reservoirs combined. Prediction error analysis was conducted in terms of the coefficient of determination (R2), root mean squared error (RMSE) and mean absolute error (MAE). The XGBoost model yielded the best porosity predictions for the individual and combined reservoirs. Prediction errors generated for reservoir T1 were (R2 ˜ 0.865, RMSE≈0.866, MAE≈0.692); for reservoir RDC were R2 ˜ 0.935, RMSE≈0.848, and MAE≈0.772; and, for reservoir QH were R2 ˜ 0.743, RMSE≈0.794, and MAE≈0.573. For the combined (multi-zone) reservoir (T1+RDC + QH), porosity predictions were less accurate, and the XGBoost model generated prediction errors of R2 ˜ 0.76, RMSE≈1.259, and MAE≈1.027.

Conventional cutting fluids are widely employed globally as coolants and lubricants in grinding operations. However, these fluids pose significant environmental and biological risks. To mitigate these adverse effects, a shift towards sustainable machining techniques is imperative. Sustainable approaches involve minimal cutting fluid usage, cryogenic cooling, utilization of vegetable oils, and the use of coated grinding wheels. The fluids employed in sustainable machining are known for their complete biodegradability and eco-friendliness. This paper provides a comprehensive review of modern sustainable techniques in metal-cutting processes. It reveals that these sustainable methods often yield superior results, including enhanced surface quality, prolonged tool life, reduced cutting temperatures, and diminished cutting forces when compared to conventional wet machining. The primary aim of this review is to identify and promote various sustainable techniques in metal machining, fostering cost-effectiveness and environmental friendliness in the grinding process.
